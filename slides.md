# 1
## What knowledge *is*

<!--

---

# Knowledge links a knower to a fact

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-10 mt-6">
<div>

Not every fact is **knowledge**. Shake a sealed box with a coin inside — it landed
heads or tails. That's a *fact*.

But while nobody looks, it sits **outside the realm of knowledge**.

Knowledge demands some kind of **access to a fact** by a living subject.

</div>
<div>

- Facts can exist unknown; **knowledge can't exist unknown**
- Everything known must be linked to a **knower**
- Knowledge belongs to an **individual or a group**
- Groups can combine members' knowledge in powerful — or destructive — ways

</div>
</div>

<!--

The group point matters later for testimony. Pacing: linger on the box. ~60s.
-->

# The starting ingredients

Three things the book pulls out before any formal theory:

| Requirement | What it means | Why it's not enough alone |
|---|---|---|
| **Truth (factivity)** | You can only know things that are *true* | A false belief is never knowledge |
| **Belief** | You have to actually *hold* the view | — |
| **The right basis** | Confidence must rest on *evidence* | The certain-but-wrong-reasons father |

The father **certain** his daughter is innocent — but only because he refuses to
look at the case — doesn't *know* it, even if she is.

# 2
## The classical recipe — and the crack in it

<!--
Divider. We're now entering the famous bit every philosophy student meets. ~8s.
-->

---

# Justified True Belief

For most of the 20th century the standard "classical analysis":

> **S knows that p if and only if**
> (i) p is true; (ii) S believes that p; (iii) S is justified in believing that p.

Clean. Intuitive. **Taught as settled.**

Then a three-page paper blew it up.

<!--
Read the definition slowly — JTB is the thing everyone "knew" was the answer. Tease
the next slide: the shortest famous demolition in philosophy. Pacing: deliberate. ~45s.
-->

---

# Gettier breaks it

Russell's **broken clock** (1948): you glance at a stopped clock that happens to read
the correct time. True belief, seemingly justified — but **not knowledge**. Just luck.

Gettier generalised the trick. **Linda Zagzebski** later gave the recipe:

1. Take a belief that is **false but justified**
2. Add a **lucky twist** so the thing believed turns out true anyway
3. → justified + true + believed, but ==still not knowledge==

The lesson: **knowledge is something more than the sum of justification and true belief.**

<!--
The broken clock is the cleanest version — use it as the hook, mention Zagzebski's
recipe as the "this works against almost any theory" generaliser. Emphasise the
takeaway line in bold. This is the engine for the next section. Pacing: ~60s.
-->

---

# A car built from scrap parts

The post-Gettier decades = patch the definition, find a new counterexample, repeat.

Nagel's image stuck with me:

> Trying to pin down knowledge from our conflicting intuitions is like identifying
> the make and model of a car **built from assorted scrap parts.**

Maybe the ordinary concept just isn't a tidy machine.

# 3
## Fixing the recipe

<!--
Divider. Two big repair strategies. Keep it moving. ~8s.
-->

---

# Reliabilism & Knowledge-First

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-10 mt-4">
<div>

### Reliabilism

Knowledge = **true belief produced by a reliable mechanism** — one *likely to produce
true beliefs*.

Shifts focus from inner justification to **how the belief was produced**.

An **externalist** move: the insight into *how* you know becomes an optional bonus,
not a requirement on every instance.

</div>
<div>

### Knowledge-First (Williamson)

Stop analysing knowledge as "belief + extra factors."

Instead, **explain belief in terms of knowing**:

> Believing p is, roughly, treating p as if one knew p.

Knowledge is the **basic** concept, not the assembled one.

</div>
</div>

Pacing: ~70s, don't get lost in the weeds.

# 4
## The sceptic in the room

<!--
Divider. The oldest worry: maybe none of this gets off the ground. ~8s.
-->

---

# Can we know anything at all?

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-10 mt-6">
<div>

- **Academic sceptics**: knowledge is *impossible*
- **Pyrrhonian sceptics**: reach *no* conclusions — suspend judgement on everything
- Stoics split **impressions** from **judgements**; because impressions are always
  fallible, the Academics pressed, knowledge fails

</div>
<div>

**Replies that stuck with me:**

- Moore's plain stand: *"Here is one hand, and here is another."*
- Some claims work like **axioms** — basic, not themselves in need of proof
- **Semantic externalism**: our very ability to *understand* the sceptic's hypothesis
  hints we're in the real world

</div>
</div>

A healthy move: **temporarily suspend** a belief to check it fits the rest of what we know.

# 5
## Where knowledge comes from

<!--
Divider. Sources — and the one the book spends real time on: other people. ~8s.
-->

---

# Testimony: knowing because someone told you

In **testimony**, someone tells you something and *the content of what they say* does
the work. Most of what you know — your birthplace, that Antarctica exists — arrives
this way.

**Locke's worry:** testimony is at best *highly probable*, never certain, so it
doesn't *literally* count as knowledge. His rational-confidence checklist:

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-2 text-xl">
<div>

1. Number of witnesses
2. Their integrity
3. Their skill

</div>
<div>

4. Their purpose in reporting
5. Internal consistency & circumstances
6. Any contrary testimony

</div>
</div>

<!--
Set up testimony as the everyday source we lean on constantly. Locke's checklist is
practical and genuinely useful — read a couple aloud. Flag the tension: he admits we
build on testimony "as firmly as if it were certain" yet denies it's knowledge. Next
slide is the pushback. Pacing: ~60s.
-->

---

# Reductionism vs the social view

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-10 mt-4">
<div>

### Reductionism

Testimony's power **reduces** to perception, memory & inference.

- **Global**: experience teaches you that testimony, in general, is reliable
- **Local**: in *each* case look for positive reasons — is she an expert? truthful
  before? plausible now?

</div>
<div>

### The pushback

If perception gives knowledge and testimony doesn't, **Locke never shows what the real
difference is.**

Understanding what someone *says* is a genuinely different way of thinking from seeing
with your own eyes — and (Craig) learning from others is how creatures survive without
experiencing everything firsthand.

</div>
</div>

A condition that stuck: to learn a fact from an informant, **the informant had better
know it themselves.**

than Locke wants. Craig's survival framing is lovely — we're built to learn from each

Pacing: ~70s.
-->

# 6
## Contextualism 
### the part I came away convinced by

<!--

~15s, let the room settle.

---

# "Know" is context-sensitive

> **Contextualism**: words like *know* and *realize* are **context-sensitive** — their
> standard shifts with the situation in which they're used.

The analogy that made it click:

Talking about scepticism **raises the standard** for "know" — just like talking about
basketball players **raises the standard** for ==tall==.

A 6-foot man is "tall" at a coffee morning and "not tall" on an NBA court. **Same man.
Same height. Different conversation.**

<!--

twice. The point: "know" isn't broken or vague, it's INDEXICAL, like "tall," "here,"
"now." Tell them this reframed the messiness from Section 2 as a feature, not a bug.
Pacing: deliberate, ~70s.
-->

---

# The mechanism: relevant alternatives

How does the standard move? Through the **field of alternatives** you must rule out.

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-10 mt-4">
<div>

- Knowing requires **discriminating** the truth from a field of alternatives (Stine)
- A **small** field → easy to count as knowing
- A **large** field (the sceptic widens it) → much harder
- **Stakes** matter too: *the more you have at stake, the more evidence you need to
  count as knowing*

</div>
<div>

**The Lee case (Nagel):**

*Low stakes* — coworker Smith just wants his jacket. Lee: "Yes, I **know** — I locked
it myself half an hour ago." Uncontroversial.

*High stakes* — same locked door, same evidence, but now **four police officers** stop
Lee. Can he still flatly say he *knows*?

Nothing about the door changed. The **context** did.

</div>
</div>

# Why this won me over

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-10 mt-6">
<div>

It **explains the mess.** Section 2's endless counterexamples make sense if "know" was
never one fixed bar to begin with.

It **matches how we actually talk.** We really do demand more before saying "know" when
it matters — a diagnosis, a verdict, or a locked door when it's the *police* asking.

</div>
<div>

It **dissolves the sceptic** instead of losing to them. The sceptic isn't proving we
never know — they're just **dragging the standard up** by making far-fetched
alternatives feel relevant.

And crucially: when you judge someone else's "know," you have to **respect their
context** — and they yours.

</div>
</div>

# The strongest objections

A good thesis earns its keep against the critiques. The ones the book raises:

| Objection | The worry |
|---|---|
| **Invariantism** | Maybe "know" *isn't* context-sensitive at all — it's absolute, and shifting "standards" is just shifting *talk*, not knowledge |
| **Closure violation** | Relevant-alternatives theory can break **Closure** — that you know whatever you correctly deduce from what you know |
| **Intuitions are shaky** | Merely *mentioning* an error makes people deny knowledge even when the person clearly isn't at risk — so are these intuitions tracking truth, or just suggestibility? |
| **Experimental challenge** | *Experimental philosophy* says: stop leaning on armchair intuitions about cases altogether |

are just manipulable). Be fair. Pacing: walk the table, ~80s.

# Holding the thesis honestly

The deepest point Nagel leaves us with:

> We don't yet fully know **which method** best reveals the nature of knowledge —
> and that's partly because we **still don't fully understand what knowledge is.**

I land on contextualism. But I hold it the way the book recommends holding any belief:
**confident enough to use, loose enough to double-check.**
